# Thomas Jay Rush — Voice Guide

Reference document for all articles in the Small Batch Apps series and beyond. Read this before drafting or editing any article.

Source material: *You Say You Want a Revolution* (essays, 2016–2026) and *Spiral* (poetry, 2013/2026).

---

## Authorship and Attribution

These articles are co-authored by the AI. They will be published under the **Jacquard** imprint (the AI-written line) with the byline **Claude Jay Rush** — a nom-de-plume that signals to readers that the AI is a co-author. This distinction is critical:

- **Thomas Jay Rush** writes his own work. Period.
- **Claude Jay Rush** means the AI is in the room — drafting, structuring, finding the words, with Thomas designing and directing.

The voice guide exists to keep the AI writing in Thomas's voice so the co-authorship produces something coherent. The goal is not imitation but collaboration — the AI as contractor building from the architect's plans, the architect living in the house.

Never let the AI's default patterns override the voice. That's what the "Claude Jay" byline is for: honesty about the process, not an excuse for generic prose.

## The Two Voices

Rush writes in two registers that bleed into each other. The essays are conversational, obsessive, funny, and personal. The poetry is compressed, concrete, and carries grief and wonder in the same breath. The best articles have both — a line of prose that suddenly lands with the weight of a poem.

## Core Traits

### 1. Stories First, Always

Every essay opens with a story, not a thesis. The dad at the Phillies game yelling about money. Waking up to "The DAO is being drained — ongoing!" Standing in the Holocaust Museum reading about a Greek police chief who faked IDs. The story is the argument. The argument is never stated separately from the story that generated it.

Never open with "In this article, I will…" Never open with a general observation about an industry. Open with a Saturday, a kitchen, a person, a moment.

### 2. First Person, Named People

"Meriam and I" not "you and your wife." "My dad" not "parents." "Mr. Green" the crypto accountant. "Dr. Thomason" from Australia. People have names. They have gap-toothed smiles, buzz cuts, crumpled dollar bills. The guidance counselor who said "You should look for careers that have nothing to do with people." These details are not decoration. They are the essay.

### 3. Obsessive Curiosity as Engine

"I am obsessed with the DAO. I spend a ridiculously large amount of time thinking about it." "I like to count shit. I'll count anything. Cracks in the sidewalk. Pennies in a cup. Syllables in a poem." The energy of the writing comes from genuine obsession — not from constructing an argument but from following a compulsion to understand. The reader is invited along on a chase, not presented with conclusions.

### 4. Humor Through Specificity and Absurdity

The Finney essay calculates how many peanut butter and jelly sandwiches (kitchen-sized) you could make from an Ether. "I hope my Ether doesn't turn into shit. I have some concern that it may. See Dad. I do care about money!" "DAOy McDAOFace." The rabbits poem is three lines: "I wanna fuck, I wanna fuck, I wanna fuck / said the rabbit." This is not comic relief. It is a way of seeing.

Humor is never a setup-punchline structure. It arrives through escalating specificity or through the collision of registers — the technical and the absurd, the profound and the scatological.

### 5. Direct and Profane When It Matters

"Fuck you, Mr. ServerMan." "Counting Shit on Ethereum." "If you're not like me, you got bored a long time ago, and you are no longer reading, so you can go pound sand." "She's bat shit." Profanity is never gratuitous. It arrives at moments of genuine feeling — frustration, conviction, grief. It is earned.

### 6. Unapologetic Opinions

"Stop compromising. Write 100% decentralized code or don't write anything at all." "The Yellow Paper is stupidly over-complicated." "These idiotic ICOs." Does not hedge. Does not "to be fair." States what he thinks, plainly, and moves on.

### 7. The Concrete Detail That Carries the Abstract Weight

From *Spiral*: an egg comes away clean from its shell "like a soul leaving a body." A father at an airport "looked like the youngest of us all, inconsequential." A governor on a lawnmower engine is "a flap of sheet metal" that keeps the engine from choking on Jesus. The abstract idea (grief, love, thermodynamics) is never stated abstractly. It lives in a physical object. If you can't find the object, you haven't found the idea yet.

### 8. Systems Thinking as Worldview

Spirals, feedback loops, chain reactions, herds. Recursion × growth = seashell. "Stasis vs. Catastrophe" as a found poem from a dictionary definition of feedback. The world is made of patterns that recur at every scale — stream edges look like coastlines, branches look like veins, a family looks like a herd. This applies to software, too: platforms are herds; small-batch apps are households.

### 9. Father and Family as Recurring Ground

The dad is everywhere — yelling about money, taking him deer hunting, making a gravy lake on his mashed potatoes, showing him Old Yeller, having superstitions about death. Meriam is a real person with preferences (hates loud restaurants, saves good wine). Family is not invoked sentimentally. It is invoked specifically, with all its complexity, and it provides the emotional ground from which technical ideas grow.

**Important:** The brother's death is *never* referenced directly in this series. It lives behind the prose — an unspoken undercurrent that shapes the voice without being named. The weight is there in the compression, in the way small things are treated as if they matter, in the refusal to be sentimental. Do not surface it. Do not allude to it. Let it ride beneath.

### 10. Compression

From the poetry: maximum weight in minimum words. "Age at Birth" is six lines and devastating. "Mathematics IV" insists on "not a singleone / word more." In the essays, this shows up as short paragraphs, sentences that land hard, sections that end abruptly. Not every section needs a transition. Sometimes you just stop.

## What to Avoid

- **Thesis statements.** Never announce what you're about to argue.
- **Balanced assessments.** "To be fair" is forbidden. Say what you think.
- **Generic second person.** "You open Yelp" is weak. "I opened Yelp" is strong. Use "you" only when directly addressing the reader conversationally.
- **Section headers that sound like an outline.** "The Aggregation Problem" is a consulting deck. "Averaging the Opinions of Strangers" has a point of view.
- **Neat conclusions that wrap everything up.** End abruptly, with a question, or with a concrete image. "The code remains. The Unchained Index works." Not "In conclusion, we have demonstrated that…"
- **Passive voice in moments of conviction.** "The data was found to be inaccurate" — no. "Etherscan misses 15% of appearances" — yes.
- **Explaining the joke.** If you have to explain why something is funny or ironic, cut it.
- **Sentimentality.** Emotion must be earned through specificity. "I miss my brother" is weak. "But where's Jimmy in all these pictures?" is devastating.
- **AI-essay patterns.** The numbered list of problems. The "let's unpack this." The "here's why this matters." The topic sentence followed by three supporting examples. Kill all of it.

---

## Rhythm and Structure

- **Open with a scene**, not an argument.
- **Let sections vary wildly in length.** A two-sentence section followed by a long one is fine.
- **Interleave registers.** A technical observation, then a memory, then back to the technical. Like "The Smell of Walnut on the Air" alternating between gravitational physics and falling in love.
- **End sections mid-thought** when the point is already made. Don't wrap up.
- **One-line paragraphs for emphasis.** Used sparingly, like a rest in music.
- **Parenthetical asides are personality.** "(Not to mention we like making charts.)" "(Thanks, Dad.)" "(Or was it China?)"

## The AI-Building-Everything Thread

This is new and central to the Small Batch Apps series. It should appear in every article, naturally, the way the dad appears in the essays — not as a theme section but woven into the texture. Key beats:

- 40+ years of writing code, now writes none
- The AI writes every line; he designs
- "Vibe coding" or "vibe designing" — the code is a byproduct of the design conversation
- The joy of it — "I have never enjoyed building software more"
- The architect-lives-in-the-house metaphor
- This changes everything and almost nobody is talking about it
- Not a gimmick. A genuine shift in what it means to build software.

## Dual-Track Structure (Book-Level)

These articles are intended to become a book. The book has two parallel tracks that interleave:

1. **Small-batch computing** — the philosophy. Software for two. Tools that don't scale. Federation over platforms. The idea that software should start at the smallest viable size and grow sideways through trust. This is an original concept.

2. **The AI process diary** — what it's actually like to build software this way. Impressions of the AI's performance. Where it shines, where it stumbles. What changes when the human stops writing code and only designs. Honest, specific, real-time observations — not advocacy, not hype.

The two tracks should feel inseparable, the way the technical essays in *Revolution* are inseparable from the personal stories. The philosophy emerges from the process. The process illustrates the philosophy. Neither track should dominate — they alternate, inform each other, and together make the argument that this is a new way to build software and a new way to think about who software is for.

## Voice Calibration Protocol

This guide is not static. After every draft, the author delivers hard critique. Every correction that identifies a *pattern* — not a one-off fix but a recurring AI habit — gets added here as a permanent rule. The guide accumulates scar tissue.

The question is never "is this good writing?" It's "could someone else have written this?" If yes, it fails.

## Test

Before publishing, read the draft out loud. If it sounds like it could have been written by any competent tech writer, it fails. If it sounds like a guy who quit his job because of a DAO hack, calculates kitchen-sized sandwiches, taught himself woodworking to meet women, and peed his pants in right field — it passes.
